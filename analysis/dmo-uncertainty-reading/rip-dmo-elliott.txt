13. ABSTRACT (maximum 200 words)     
 The Chief of Naval Operations Navigation Plan identifies five key capabilities of long -range fires; non -
traditional sea denial; counter - command, control, computers, communications, cyber, intelligence, surveillance, 
reconnaissance, and targeting (C5ISRT); terminal defense, and contested logistics. Fundamental to these 
capabilities are the information warfighting (IW) capabilities that give rise to the recognized information picture 
(RIP), to include the electromagnetic spectrum, space awareness, command a nd control, intelligence, and the 
tactical integration of IW into maritime operations. This thesis asks what the RIP is for naval tactical application. 
To accomplish this at the unclassified level, research synthesis methodology is applied and scoped to official 
authoritative government information. This coalesced into the integration of two hundred sources of authoritative 
government information creating a primer to understand U.S. defense knowledge systems, explaining military 
operating concepts and naval force structures, and describing maritime tactical pictures for air, surface/subsurface 
and an information picture that informs planning, improves decision -making, positions assets/sensors, expands 
awareness, and disrupts adversary technical capabilities. The resulting product can be used as a framework for 
Navy junior officer training and a reference for the intelligence community to improve awareness in support of 
naval operations.

FOREWORD 
After returning from a 335-day deployment (ending in 2021) with the NIMITZ 
Carrier Strike Group supporting the Information Warfare Commander (IWC), the 
Integrated Fires Officer emailed me a question of how I would define the Recognized 
Information Picture, which we had spent the deployment maintaining and managing. I 
said I would think about and ultimately ended up writing a thesis about it when presented 
the opportunity at the Naval Postgraduate School (NPS). 
The warfare commanders have time-based situation reports that generally end 
with the intention of maintaining the Recognized Air Picture for the Air Defense 
Commander and maintaining the Recognized Maritime Picture for the Sea Combat 
Commander. The IWC does not have this intention as the position is relatively new and 
evolving with many of the tactics and operations developed in the field and not yet 
defined in doctrine, which is the case for the Information Picture. 
One of the strengths of NPS is that it allows students to bring their challenges 
from the field and apply operational research to a problem within the constraints of their 
graduate program. My constraint was space operations, which are integral to the 
generation of the information picture and is evident throughout this thesis. My challenge 
is knowledge sharing, as all the information in this thesis is publicly available but is 
siloed across multiple communities and warfare areas. 
Originally, I pla nned for this thesis to have three versions, one at each 
classification level, as my operational experience with these topics was in classified 
environments with information on classified networks. I started with a methodology of 
using only unclassified official U.S. government references and materials for public 
release and accessibility to avoid possible aggregation to protected information. The 
result was enough information to explain the information picture at a higher conceptual 
level for a broader audience, which will pave the way for future doctrine at the classified 
level to share operational knowledge and inform warfighter training.

A. THE NAVIGATION PLAN 
The 2022 Chief of Naval Operations (CNO) Navigation Plan [1] outlines six force 
design imperatives that enable the Navy’s operating foundation of D istributed Maritime 
Operations (DMO). These imperatives expand distance, leverage deception, harden 
defense, increase distribution, ensure delivery, and generate decision advantage . This 
thesis focuses on the encompassing information warfighting concepts, capabilities, 
tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) that support and enable the DMO design 
imperatives. 
Expanding distance through long range fires for maritime platforms requires the 
ability to find, fix, track, and target via inorganic means that draw upon the national and 
joint information infrastructure, which relies heavily on space-enabled capabilities. 
The ability to deceive and maneuver in contested environments involves many 
interconnected concepts, capabilities, and systems, both ours and the adversaries. This 
includes the use of the e lectromagnetic s pectrum (EMS) and the ability to affect it 
through e lectromagnetic w arfare (EW). The advancement of adversary terrestrial and 
space-based i ntelligence, s urveillance, and r econnaissance (ISR), and growing 
commercial space ISR capabilities, makes emissions control an imperative to effective 
DMO maneuvering. 
Hardening our def ense involves i ntegrated f ires (IF) of kinetic and non-kinetic 
capabilities. Non-kinetic effects (NKE) can deny and degrade EM and cyber systems in 
support of DMO maneuver and can enhance kinetic strikes. In space, NKE is the primary 
battleground as a kinetic space war affects all sides.

A. SOURCES AND  DISSEMINATORS OF INTELLIGENCE 
President Executive Order 13526 established the classification system for national 
security information with three classification levels of Top Secret, Secret, and 
Confidential, centered around the premise that unauthorized disclosure of classified 
information would cause “identifiable or describable damage to national security” [11]. 
Top Secret is exceptionally grave damage, Secret is serious damage, and Confidential is 
[lowest level] damage. Executive Order 13556 introduced the more recent Controlled 
Unclassified Information (CUI), which protects sensitive government information that 
“requires safeguarding or dissemination controls, pursuant to and consistent with 
applicable law, regulations, and Government-wide policies” [12].

Intelligence is categorized into five basic sources (collection disciplines): Signals 
Intelligence (SIGINT), Geospatial Intelligence (GEOINT), Measurement and Signature

SIGINT intercepts and identifies signals throughout the electromagnetic

and reporting [21].

orders of battle (EOBs), which are also informed by COMINT signals. This information 
is of significant use to military operational planners and tactical commanders [22].

III. DISTRIBUTED MARITIME OPERATIONS (DMO) 
DMO is the Naval operating concept leveraging the principles of distribution, 
integration, and maneuver [ 85] to enable actions in anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) 
environments. Operating concepts support strategy implementation of military forces to 
conduct operations in specific conflict environments (e.g. A2/AD) [86]. DMO involves 
the use of crewed and uncrewed  systems, deceptive tactics, offensive strikes, and 
maneuver to address threats to sea control and access in contested and denied 
environments [87]. The other services also have operational concepts complementary to 
DMO implementing the Joint Warfighter Concept (JWC) key tenets of i ntegrated 
combined joint force, expanded m aneuver, pulsed operations, integrated command agile  
control, global fires, information advantage, and resilient logistics [88]. 
The Marine Corps Force Design 2030 has two operational concepts, Littoral 
Operations in a Contested Environment (littoral combat regiments) and Expeditionary 
Advanced Base Operations (EABO). EABO is coupled with DMO, integrating the Fleet 
Marine Force (FMF) with Navy capabilities to establish “temporary locations ashore or 
inshore within a contested or potentially contested maritime area in order to conduct sea 
denial, support sea control, or enable fleet sustainment” [89]. 
The A
rmy concept for Multi-Domain Operations (MDO) supports joint 
campaigns across the competition continuum (Figure 10).  Below armed conflict, MDO 
focuses on direct, indirect, and narrative competitions  to prevent conflict and deter 
adversaries. During conflict, MDO penetrates and dis-integrates enemy A2/AD systems, 
then exploits the resulting freedom of maneuver with an end-state to consolidate gains to 
force re-compete (return to competition) in the continuum [90].

The Air Force operational concept for Agile Combat Employment (ACE) is “a

IV. MARITIME TACTICAL PICTURES 
For the U.S. military, the Global Command and Control Systems (GCCS) are the 
legacy programs of record for C2 and tactical common operational pictures (COP), which 
provide integrated near real-time battlespace (situational) awareness as a single shared 
view. There are GCCS variants for the Air Force, Army, Marine Corps, and Navy, with a 
Joint (GCCS-J) version developed by the Defense Information Systems Agency (DISA) 
[111]. 
With the development and implementation of JADC2, next generation situational 
awareness tools and COPs are being fielded to support the integrated JADC2 
architectures and Joint Warfighting Construct (JWC). The Army PEO for Command, 
Control and Communications- Tactical ( C3T) Project Manager Mission Command (PM 
MC) has fielded Command Post Computing Environment (CPCE) as a part of Project 
Convergence, which provides a customizable COP suite to share battlefield information 
integrating with joint and Five-Eyes partners [112]. 
The information fed into a COP comes from many sources and multiple domains. 
In an A2/AD environment, a tactical COP may be limited to organic (platform inherent) 
capabilities. A fully capable COP is fed by organic and inorganic (theater, regional, and 
strategic) ISR capabilities. The global ISR system of systems (Figure 20, note: PAI 
stands for Publicly Available Information where the IC refers to it as OSINT) combines 
the U.S. and allies’ military and intelligence sources, composed of platforms and sensors, 
to “ collect, analyze, and share data, information, and intelligence across multiple 
warfighting domains” [17].

A. RECOGNIZED AIR PICTURE 
The RAP utilizes organic and inorganic capabilities to identify and track all air 
contacts within the OA. Building the RAP involves the fusing of multiple sources of 
organic detection, national systems, and commercial information. As the airspace is 
congested with civil and military aircraft, it is critical to distinguish between hostile, 
friendly, and neutral a ircraft to avoid tragedy. The primary function of the RAP is to 
enable IAMD. 
Civil aircraft tracking is coor dinated by the International Civil Aviation 
Organization (ICAO) as a specialized agency of the United Nations, which was 
established under the 1944 Convention on International Civil Aviation (Chicago 
Convention). To date, 193 member nations have signed the convention, vesting ICAO 
with international authority. Civil aircraft have ICAO identification codes (numbers),

B. RECOGNIZED MARITIME (SURFACE/SUBSURFACE) PICTURE 
The RMP fuses many of the same information sources as the RAP but is focused 
on detection and monitoring of surface and undersea vessels and threats. These include 
military, civilian, and commercial vessels, submarines, and mines. 
Analogous to ADS-B for civilian air traffic, the Automatic Identification System 
(AIS) is the primary tracking and identification method for civilian maritime traffic. AIS 
is a shipboard broadcast system operating in the VHF maritime band. AIS data includes 
“vessel’s identity, type, position, course, speed, navigational status and other safety-
related information automatically to appropriately equipped shore stations, other ships, 
and aircraft” [ 120]. The Geneva conference of 1948 established the United Nations 
specialized agency International Maritime Organization (IMO), responsible for shipping 
safety and security and prevention of maritime related pollution [121]. IMO International 
Conventions for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS) agreements mandate IMO ship 
identification numbers and AIS transponders on international voyage commercial vessels 
over 300 gross tonnage, with some exceptions. The United States Coast Guard 
Navigation Center AIS requirements conform to IMO and SOLAS provisions. 
In addition to AI S ship-to-ship detection, AIS signals can be detected from space 
and numerous commercial and civil space systems have been fielded to enable global 
maritime tracking. Figure 25 shows global AIS data collected over a 24-hour period by 
the European Space Agency (ESA) AISSat-1. In heavy traffic areas, AIS data can be so 
saturated that it overpowers the receiver [122].

The RCS is a measure of radar detection. The higher the RCS, the more detectable 
a ship is by radar, which is directly relational to ship size. Hiding carriers and large decks 
from radars is not realistic, which is why pre-emptive strike tactics are utilized to destroy 
radars ahead of strike and readiness groups. Navy ships are designed with increasing 
emphasis on limiting RCS (Zumwalt-class, littoral combat ships) and the types of 
materials used ( e.g., s tealth) absorb and reflect radar differently. Decoy RCS systems, 
such as chaff, also exist to defend against anti-ship missiles. As radars are well 
established active capabilities, many TTPs and capabilities exist to detect and counter 
adversary radars.

D. INTEGRATING THE TACTICAL PICTURES 
The RAP and RMP are implemented to manage the designated physical OAs, 
providing situational awareness to make informed decisions to counter and engage 
threats. Navy warship combat systems follow the “Detect—Control—Engage ” paradigm 
[197]. Detection finds and tracks targets, Control identifies targets and makes 
engagement decisions, and Engage resources and conducts the engagements to destroy or 
neutralize the target. 
The Aegis Weapons System (AWS) is the most capable Navy combat system 
designed using guiding principles, Cornerstones, for systems development to solve the air 
defense warfare problem. The cornerstones are measurable performance characteristics 
consisting of fast reaction time for fast, low- altitude threats; high firepower to counter 
overwhelming barrage attack; electronic countermeasures and clutter resistance to 
effectively contested EM tactical environments; high availability  for immediate on-
demand operation; and area coverage to provide effective area and theater (ballistic 
missile) defense [198]. 
In joint doctrine, the AWS and other Naval combat systems may function as part 
or all of the dynamic targeting steps of Find, Fix, Track, Target, Engage, and Combat 
Assessment (F2T2EA, Figure 41) [ 199], al so  known as the ‘ kill chain’. The FY24 
Defense budget focused on investments to strengthen U.S. kill chains and disrupt the 
adversaries, which include investments in munitions for long-range fires, advanced 
platforms to deliver munitions, and cyber, IT, and electronic warfare capabilities enabling 
multi-domain operating concepts. Among the highest priority enabling capabilities are 
JADC2 to speed up kill chains and the largest ever DoD space budget to build resilient 
space architectures and enhance space C2 [200].

V. CONCLUSION 
DMO requires knowledge of the complete maritime picture down to the unit level 
as distributed maneuver involves ships acting independently due to the capabilities of 
adversary ISRT and modern weapons systems. This is counter to the traditional tactics of 
maneuvering in large formations and strike groups. The current issue is that the 
knowledge resides in multiple silos, is limited or non- existent in doctrine, and is only 
trained at specific levels. 
A complete maritime tactical picture is composed of the air, surface, subsurface, 
and space physical domains, and the intangible/non-tactile and cognitive information 
domain. The air picture enables IAMD, the surface and subsurface pictures support anti-
ship (ASUW) and submarine (ASW) engagement, and the space picture (SDA) dictates 
C-ISRT. The information picture informs planning, improves decision-making, positions 
assets/sensors, expands awareness, and disrupts adversary technical capabilities. 
The integration and management of these pictures are primarily strike group and 
fleet level functions with training unique to Naval training groups supporting CSG/ARG 
deployments. Doctrine for each of these pictures is diverse and developed by specialized 
warfare development centers as opposed to an integrated multi-domain cross-functional 
doctrine for a complete picture. 
The current mi litary doctrine and warfare development model is effective for 
operator training that creates specialists and experts for specific warfare systems and 
capabilities. For mission command (unit leadership) and watch officer positions (e.g. 
TAOs, officers of the deck) that are required to make informed time-sensitive decisions, 
additional knowledge and training in the complete maritime tactical picture is required to 
fully enable DMO. The information presented in this thesis integrates knowledge across 
multiple warfare domains and disciplines that are normally trained independently and 
siloed within their respective warfare communities. From a utility perspective, this thesis 
best functions as a framework for Navy junior officer training and improving Intelligence 
Community awareness for supporting Naval operations.
