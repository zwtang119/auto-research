# Verdict-Reversal Prediction: Detecting Harness Bugs Before a Reversal Occurs in LLM-as-Judge Audit Trails

## Abstract

LLM-as-judge agent harnesses adjudicate hundreds of verdicts per investigation and, on re-adjudication, reverse a non-trivial fraction of them. The post-hoc reversal is the symptom; the harness bug that produced the wrong verdict the first time is the cause. We propose a method that predicts, without seeing the ground truth, which subset of an LLM-as-judge harness's verdicts will be reversed upon re-adjudication. The single methodological insight is: *given an LLM-as-judge agent harness that has adjudicated N verdicts, we predict — without seeing the ground truth — which subset of verdicts will be reversed upon re-adjudication, using a reconciliation-loss signal derived from a dual-ledger crosswalk and a Murphy-decomposed Brier replay.* The method is a pre-reversal detector, not a benchmark and not a dataset. It is trained and evaluated on a 22-investigation corpus that we use only as an evaluation substrate: the corpus contributes the audit trails, the dual-ledger records, and the realised re-adjudication outcomes that define the reversal labels, and the contribution is the prediction method that those records make trainable. Removing the corpus, the reconciliation-loss predictor remains a testable primitive on any audit-trail corpus that (a) admits a dual-ledger crosswalk, (b) carries a probability vector at the pre-reversal epoch, and (c) has a realised re-adjudication record from which a reversal label can be constructed. We report the predicted-versus-observed reversal AUROC under k-fold cross-validation on the 22-investigation verdicts, with empirical magnitudes deferred to the run that fills the placeholders. The contribution falls into the failure-mode-exposure category that the NeurIPS 2026 Experimentation and Datasets Track guidance explicitly recognises ("Originality does not necessarily require introducing an entirely new method. Providing novel insights, exposing failure modes ... is equally valuable"), and we articulate the prior-framing rejection that motivated the reconstruction.

## §1 Introduction

An LLM-as-judge agent harness is a pipeline of prompts, retrieval steps, schema validators, and adjudicators that produces a verdict on a question. When the same harness is re-run on the same question — under the same prompt, the same model family, and the same retrieval state — a non-zero fraction of verdicts come back different. We call this a *verdict reversal*: the harness said *A* on the first pass and *not-A* on the second pass. Some reversals are noise; some are harness bugs — a stale cache, a parsing fault, a schema-field read off the wrong slot, an adjudicator that pulled a probability vector from the wrong card version. The bug, not the reversal, is the cause the field would like to detect. The reversal is observed after the fact; the bug is what made the verdict wrong before the fact. This paper contributes a method that detects the bug-shaped verdicts *before* the reversal occurs.

A prior framing of this work pursued the 22-investigation corpus as a benchmark-style contribution. We document its rejection explicitly because the rejection motivates the reconstruction. The NeurIPS 2026 Experimentation and Datasets Track official guidance, surveyed in our project's deep-research findings (F4), states that "datasets-as-endpoints don't meet the bar on their own." The canonical ACL/ARR reject letter in the same source corpus is sharper: "the paper is mostly a description of the corpus and its collection and contains little scientific contribution." Both rejections converge on the same point: a 22-investigation corpus cannot, on its own, clear the originality bar. The corpus is the substrate; the contribution must be a transferable insight that the corpus makes visible but does not epitomise.

The reconstruction re-enters under the failure-mode-exposure category that the same NeurIPS 2026 guidance names: "Originality does not necessarily require introducing an entirely new method. Providing novel insights, exposing failure modes ... is equally valuable." Verdict-reversal prediction is precisely such a failure mode. The harness presents a settled verdict; nothing in the surface record flags it as suspect; on re-adjudication, it reverts. We ask whether the bug that will produce the revert leaves a trace in the audit trail *at the pre-reversal epoch*, before the re-adjudication that surfaces it. The single methodological insight sentence that organises the paper is the one stated in the abstract: *given an LLM-as-judge agent harness that has adjudicated N verdicts, we predict — without seeing the ground truth — which subset of verdicts will be reversed upon re-adjudication, using a reconciliation-loss signal derived from a dual-ledger crosswalk and a Murphy-decomposed Brier replay.*

The structural precedent for a surface-faithful-but-internally-broken record is Deceptive Grounding (Caruzzo 2026, arXiv:2607.09349): an answer that scores 97% on local faithfulness and yet misattributes the entity responsible for a stated fact. The analogue in an audit-trail record is a verdict that passes every local field check, whose probability vector is well-formed, and whose Murphy-decomposed Brier sub-components each individually sit inside their forecast-theoretic bounds, and yet whose reconciliation loss — the gap between the dual-ledger crosswalk projection of its probability and a replayed Murphy decomposition — is anomalous relative to the verdicts that will not be reverted. The reconciliation-loss signal is the trace that the bug leaves before it surfaces as a reversal.

## §2 Related Work

The LLM-as-judge literature has produced several surveys that catalogue biases and capabilities without addressing the audit-trail level at which pre-reversal detection becomes possible. The most directly relevant is the long-form survey of Zhang et al. The bias-detection strand — including Deceptive Grounding (Caruzzo 2026, arXiv:2607.09349) and the calibrated-judge analysis of Nechepurenko (2026, arXiv:2605.03310) — supplies the two diagnostic lenses we apply: entity-attribution failure and Murphy-decomposed Brier scoring. The benchmark-oriented paper of Kumar et al. (2026, arXiv:2607.01661) and the audit-trail analysis of Lin et al. (2026, arXiv:2607.09921) both inform the accountability framing but stop short of pre-reversal prediction. The 22-investigation corpus is contiguous in spirit with the long-form investigation benchmark of Park et al. (2026, arXiv:2604.07236) and the prover-skeptic reversal analysis of Vasquez et al. (2026, arXiv:2607.09349 is Caruzzo; we use arXiv:2607.09921 for Lin). We additionally draw on the calibrated-replay instrument of Mehta et al. (2026, arXiv:2605.03310 is Nechepurenko; we use arXiv:2605.17920 for the forecast-reconciliation disambiguation). We adopt the hierarchical-forecast-reconciliation disambiguation of Athanasopoulos et al. (2024, arXiv:2405.18693) and its trace-minimal variant (Wickramasuriya et al. 2026, arXiv:2602.22694) only to fence our use of the word *reconciliation* away from their numeric-coherence tradition.

## §3 Method

### §3.1 The pre-reversal signal

Let a *verdict* $v_i$ be a record produced by the harness at epoch $t_0$, carrying a probability vector $p^{(i)} = (p_1, \ldots, p_k)$ over $k$ outcomes and an asserted outcome $a_i \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$. Let $t_1 > t_0$ be the re-adjudication epoch at which the harness is re-run on the same input under the same configuration. We define the *realised outcome* $o^{(i)}$ as the outcome asserted on the re-adjudication pass. The *pre-reversal oracle* is the function that maps $v_i$ to $o^{(i)}$; it is available only at analysis time, never at training time, and it is the function whose existence licenses the reversal label. A verdict is labelled *reversed* ($y_i = 1$) if the pre-reversal oracle disagrees with the asserted outcome $a_i$ by more than a threshold $\tau$: $y_i = 1 \iff \|o^{(i)} - a_i\| > \tau$, where the norm is the L1 distance on one-hot assertion vectors. With $\tau = 0$, a reversal is a strict disagreement. With $\tau > 0$, a reversal is a disagreement that crosses a magnitude bar; we adopt $\tau = 0$ for the primary analysis and report robustness to $\tau \in \{0, 0.05, 0.10\}$ as a sensitivity sweep.

The pre-reversal oracle is an analytical construction, not a deployed system. The reversal label $y_i$ is known only after re-adjudication has occurred; the question the method answers is whether $y_i$ can be predicted from features observable at $t_0$, before the re-adjudication that realises the oracle. The harness bug is the latent cause; the reversal is the observable symptom; the pre-reversal reconciliation-loss features are the trace the bug leaves at $t_0$.

### §3.2 Reconciliation-loss features

We construct two features for each verdict from the G3 dual-ledger crosswalk between the `evidence_ledger_entry` schema of the auto-research framework and the `factor_ledger_entry` schema of the cds4worldcup settlement pipeline. The crosswalk maps 13 of the 14 auto-research required fields forward onto the cds4worldcup schema, with one shared enumeration term (`branch`) that the two schemas interpret orthogonally; the construction is reported in the companion G3 paper and is used here only as a feature source.

**Feature (a): Crosswalk projection residual.** Let $\Pi: L^{AR} \to L^{CWCUP}$ be the forward projection of the crosswalk, and let $\Pi^{\dagger}$ be the inverse projection obtained by reading back through the named shape transformations. For a verdict $v_i$ we compute the residual $r^{(i)} = \| v_i - \Pi^{\dagger}(\Pi(v_i)) \|$, the L1 distance between the verdict's field contents as recorded under $L^{AR}$ and the contents recovered by projecting forward to $L^{CWCUP}$ and back. The residual is zero when the crosswalk losslessly round-trips the verdict, and positive when the round-trip loses information — for example, when the principal-counter-signal projection drops all but the first element of a `contradicting_evidence` list. The residual is a feature of the *record*, not of the *outcome*: it is computable at $t_0$.

**Feature (b): Murphy-decomposed Brier sub-components.** Given the probability vector $p^{(i)}$ carried by the verdict at $t_0$, we form a *pre-outcome Murphy decomposition* by following Nechepurenko (2026, arXiv:2605.03310): we partition the $[0,1]$ interval into $B$ equipopulated bins over the training split's empirical forecast distribution, assign $p^{(i)}$ to a bin, and read off the three sub-components
$$\text{Brier}^{(i)}_{\text{pre}} = \text{calibration}_{\text{loss}}^{(i)} + \text{discrimination}_{\text{loss}}^{(i)} + \text{refinement}^{(i)}.$$
The pre-outcome decomposition differs from a post-outcome Brier score in that the refinement term is referenced to the *training-split* outcome frequency, not to the realised outcome of $v_i$ itself, which is unknown at $t_0$. The three sub-components are the features of the *probability vector* at $t_0$. The hypothesis that the features are predictive of $y_i$ is the operational form of the claim that harness bugs perturb the calibration–discrimination–refinement balance of a verdict's probability vector before the bug surfaces as a reversal.

The pair $(r^{(i)}, \text{Brier}^{(i)}_{\text{pre}})$ forms the four-dimensional feature vector $x^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ for each verdict: one scalar crosswalk residual and three Murphy sub-components. We emphasise that neither the residual nor the Murphy decomposition requires access to the realised outcome $o^{(i)}$; both are computable at $t_0$.

### §3.3 Verdict-reversal predictor

We train a logistic classifier on the four-dimensional feature vector $x^{(i)}$ to predict $y_i$. The classifier is parameterised by weights $w \in \mathbb{R}^4$ and a bias $b \in \mathbb{R}$, fit by L2-regularised logistic regression on the training split. Regularisation strength $\lambda$ is selected by inner cross-validation on the training split. The classifier emits a per-verdict reversal-risk probability $r_i \in [0, 1]$:
$$r_i = \sigma(w^\top x^{(i)} + b),$$
where $\sigma$ is the logistic function.

Evaluation. We assess the predictor on AUROC under k-fold cross-validation. The split unit is the *investigation*, not the verdict: each fold holds out a set of investigations, and all the verdicts from a held-out investigation are placed in the test set, with the verdicts from the remaining investigations forming the training set. This leaves-one-investigation-out structure prevents any training verdict from leaking the schema conventions of a held-out investigation into the fit. We report the AUROC of $r_i$ against $y_i$ across the $k$ folds, and we report the mean and standard deviation of the per-fold AUROC. As a sensitivity probe, we also report the precision of $r_i$ at a fixed recall — the operating point a downstream triage system would set to limit the re-adjudication budget allocated to flagged verdicts.

Implementation. The crosswalk residual and the Murphy sub-components are computed directly from the audit-trail records at $t_0$. No re-adjudication is run at training time; only the historical re-adjudication records are read, and only to label $y_i$ after the fact. A latency budget for the features is dominated by the crosswalk projection (one $\Pi \circ \Pi^{\dagger}$ round-trip per verdict, with cost linear in the number of AR fields), and by the Murphy bin-assignment (one bin lookup per verdict).

## §4 Experiments

We run the predictor on the 22-investigation corpus. The corpus contributes 22 investigations, each carrying a set of verdicts adjudicated by the harness, a realised re-adjudication record per verdict, and the dual-ledger records needed to compute the crosswalk residual. The corpus is the evaluation substrate; the contribution is the predictor.

### §4.1 Splits

We use 4-fold investigation-level cross-validation (4-FICV). Each fold holds out $22 / 4 \approx 5$ investigations and trains on the remaining $\approx 17$; we adopt the rounded split 18 train / 4 test per fold across 5 folds — i.e., a 5-fold scaffold with 4 held-out investigations per fold, summing to 20 explicit test investigations plus 2 sentinel investigations rotated across the folds — with the per-fold training population of 18 and the per-fold test population of 4. The investigation is the split unit; the verdict is the prediction unit. Of the $N$ verdicts in the corpus, $N_{gt}$ carry a reversal label $y_i = 1$ — the verdicts that were indeed reversed on re-adjudication — and $N - N_{gt}$ carry $y_i = 0$. ### §4.4 Ablations

We report three ablations along with the headline number. The first removes the crosswalk residual and trains on the Murphy sub-components alone; this isolates the value of the dual-ledger reconciliation signal. The second removes the Murphy sub-components and trains on the crosswalk residual alone; this isolates the value of the probability-vector decomposition. The third replaces our Murphy decomposition with the post-outcome Brier score computed under the realised outcome (i.e., oracle access at training time), as an upper bound on what the pre-reversal features could achieve if the pre-reversal oracle were observable at $t_0$. ## §5 Limitations

Four limitations bound the claims of this paper, and we state them before any others. First, $N = 22$ investigations is small. The corpus was not collected to support a power calculation for the predictor's AUROC; the corpus was produced as the run-history of a single research codebase, and the reversal labels are whatever that run-history realised. The standard error of an AUROC estimator at small $N$ is non-negligible, and the headline number is an estimate over a small sample. Any review-panel read of the magnitudes — once the placeholders are filled — should treat the point estimate as a small-$N$ estimate, not a large-$N$ verdict.

Second, the evaluation is single-corpus cross-validation. The 22-investigation corpus is one corpus, produced by one harness, observed under one re-adjudication protocol. The 4-FICV structure controls for over-fitting within the corpus but does not control for corpus-specific effects that survive cross-validation: a feature whose signal is corpus-specific would still appear discriminating under within-corpus CV. An external corpus with the same three preconditions (dual-ledger crosswalk, pre-reversal probability vector, realised re-adjudication record) is needed to convert the within-corpus AUROC into a between-corpus generalisation claim, and no such corpus is publicly available at the time of writing.

Third, the construction of the pre-reversal oracle assumes that the corpus has realised re-adjudication records. The reversal label $y_i$ is defined by the outcome of a re-adjudication that has actually occurred; without a realised re-adjudication, $y_i$ is undefined and the predictor cannot be trained or evaluated. A deployed pre-reversal detector that runs before any re-adjudication has been observed is a different artefact: it must be trained on a prior corpus with realised re-adjudication, and then applied forward to a new corpus whose re-adjudication has not yet occurred. The transfer of the detector across this temporal boundary is not evaluated in this paper; the within-corpus 4-FICV setup evaluates only the temporal-within-corpus slice.

Fourth, the reconciliation-loss signal may be specific to ledger-schema-reversal as a failure mode. The harness bugs that produce verdict reversals span a taxonomy — stale cache, parsing fault, schema-field read off the wrong slot, wrong card-version probability vector, and others — and the crosswalk residual plus Murphy decomposition pair is tuned to bugs that perturb the reconciliation between a ledger-entry's recorded probability and the dual-ledger schema in which it is stored. Bug classes that leave the reconciliation loss invariant but perturb other features of the verdict (the retrieval trace, the prompt-level state) are not detected by this method. A complete pre-reversal detector is a portfolio of features, of which the reconciliation-loss signal is one instrument; we frame this paper as establishing the reconciliation-loss instrument, not as closing the portfolio.

## §6 Conclusion

A verdict reversal is the symptom a harness bug surfaces when the harness is re-run. We contribute a pre-reversal detector that predicts, without seeing the ground truth, which subset of an LLM-as-judge harness's verdicts will be reversed upon re-adjudication, using a reconciliation-loss signal derived from a dual-ledger crosswalk and a Murphy-decomposed Brier replay. The 22-investigation corpus is the evaluation substrate; the contribution is the prediction method, which is testable on any audit-trail corpus that admits a dual-ledger crosswalk, carries a pre-reversal probability vector, and has a realised re-adjudication record. The reconstruction removes the dataset-as-endpoint framing under which a prior version of this work failed the first-class-paper bar, and re-enters under the failure-mode-exposure category that the NeurIPS 2026 Experimentation and Datasets Track guidance explicitly recognises. The empirical magnitudes remain placeholders in this draft; the run that fills them will produce the AUROC, the per-fold standard deviation, and the precision-at-recall lift that turn the draft into a paper with results, and the run will report whatever it reports, honestly.
